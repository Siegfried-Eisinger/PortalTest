1. General

Structure and style manual for DNV GL service documents, forms and templates.

1.1. Introduction

The adoption of digital technologies is currently picking up speed in all industries and throughout society at large. Assets transform from pure physical assets to also becoming digital assets, either as cyber-physical (mixed) assets or as pure digital assets (purely software-based). For example, modern cars contain more than 100 computers and many inter-linked networks. Communication patterns in society are currently undergoing tremendous changes, which will continue into the foreseeable future. These transformations are driven by the opportunities they promise and which often seem needed to manage current challenges. On the other hand the transformations introduce complexities and new risks, which shall be managed to avoid unacceptable side effects, or even malicious activities. As automation and simulation transform the way systems are being designed and operated, they dictate a transformation in also the way we do assurance and generate trust in these systems. This relation is not optional. Recent accidents with partially autonomous vehicles or with new aircraft types demonstrate that assurance of digital assets is a mandate: a fundamental prerequisite to ensure safety and establish trust in new technologies.

One important way of analysing complex, digital assets with respect to risks is through simulation models, i.e. capturing the most essential elements of a system and its intended usage, modelling the most important interactions between the elements and thus facilitating better understanding of complexities, identifying and controlling unwanted side effects and optimising the system towards acceptable usage and risk. The definition and introduction of assurance activities to establish trust in simulation models is the objective of this recommended practice (RP).

A wide variety of simulation models are used for technical and non-technical systems. A few examples illustrate that:

  • discrete event models to optimize safety and performance within sectors like transportation, oil and gas, process industry and shipping
  • process models (modelling e.g. fluid flow, temperature and electrical current) to design and optimize equipment
  • control models to develop and test control systems and -strategies at an early stage and reduce later integration problems
  • stress/strength models to optimise material usage in construction and its structural strength
  • logistic models to optimise schedules and adapt to operational disturbances
  • societal models to predict e.g. the spread of diseases
  • environmental models, ranging from local scale (e.g. iceberg drift or wind turbine usage) to global scale in climate change models.

The usage of simulation models is increasing and efforts to combine component models from various vendors into system models are becoming commonplace. These efforts are driven by the need to manage large and complex systems and will increase in the future. Simulation models are also an important ingredient in digital twins.

1.2. Objective

Leveraging simulation models to generate trust in complex systems requires that the simulation model itself can be trusted. The definition and introduction of assurance activities to establish this trust in simulation models is the objective of this recommended practice (RP). In other words, what is necessary in terms of competence and assurance activities so that the risk of misleading simulation model results is minimized to an acceptable level. See also Trust-in-AI (see Table 1-2), which includes a more detailed definition and discussion of trust.

Mandatory
  • Short statement of the purpose or objective of the document.
  • Specific result(s) that the document aims to achieve.
  • This shall serve as the basis for why the document was established.

1.3. Scope

The scope of this RP covers the provision of a framework for assuring simulation models, depending on the risk for misleading simulation results.

This RP covers simulation models based on first principles as defined in Table 1-5. Data-driven models are not included in this RP, but in the separate DNVGL-RP-0510 Framework for assurance of data-driven algorithms and models.

The framework includes the following activities:

  1. Risk evaluation of the application of the simulation model and identification of a required confidence level from this analysis.
  2. Supplier assurance, where the maturity level of the model supplier is established and compared with the required confidence level. This procedure may be called process related.
  3. Model assurance, representing the main assurance activities and advocate product assurance of the model itself. Model assurance includes qualification of the conceptual model, verification of the simulation model and validation of the simulation model towards the reality, see Figure 2-1.

The recommended practice advocates re-use of component models. Such component models should be pre-assured.

Mandatory
  • The subject matter of the document and aspects covered and not covered.
  • Shall not contain requirements.
  • Should be brief and to the point.

2. Concepts and terminology

2.1. Reality, conceptual model and simulation model

Simulation models map selected aspects of reality into the digital world, for a given usage and for stated users. In order to assure this mapping, it is necessary to establish a conceptual model as a first step of abstracting from the reality towards a model. The conceptual model shall establish a sound understanding of the features to be modelled, the (mathematical) concepts of how to model these features and the high-level architecture of the model. Only then it is possible to construct the digital asset of the simulation model. All three elements, the reality, the conceptual model and the simulation model are important and are essential to be included in the assurance of a simulation model, providing trust in the model and the predictions it makes about the reality in a specific use case. This relationship between reality, the conceptual model and the simulation model is shown in Figure 2-1, together with the general assurance activities, denoted as qualification, verification and validation.

Relationship between reality, conceptual model and simulation model, together with verification and validation activities. Based on (Schlesinger, 1979), see Table 4-4

Assurance of a simulation model calls for conceptual model qualification towards the reality, verification of the simulation model towards the conceptual model and validation of the simulation model towards the reality. For example, only performing model verification will cut both bonds towards reality and leaves the modeller with a (verified) abstract model, not useable for trustworthy decision support. If simulation models are used over time, the procedure becomes iterative in many ways, due to for example new knowledge becoming available, changed model usages or changes in the reality. The assurance process shall accommodate that.
